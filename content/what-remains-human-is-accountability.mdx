## Automation and accountability as a core human trait

Most of white collar work doesn't require thinking. Most of the work they do is the type of work that humans have always automated — the repetitive, dull, and mindless ones. Neither white nor blue collar jobs are spared from the pursuit of automation. Artificial Intelligence (AI) can automate most modern knowledge work tasks. Yet, AI can't automate the willingness to stake your name on a decision. You can't sue an AI. You can't fire it. You can't shame it.

We've automated everything from farming, to construction, manufacturing, mining, and every industry built on repetitive human labor. Automation isn't limited to just physical labor. We've automated mental labor as well, from arithmetic, to bookkeeping, drafting, filing, analyzing and every profession where humans were the processors.

A worker has a job to do tasks, which are the atomic units of a job. The job of a factory worker still exists 200 years later, yet the tasks have changed. Many of the laborious tasks have been automated, new ones such as managing robots, have emerged. What will the workers of the future do? They will have the same job, e.g., consultant, but with different tasks.

The automation of tasks happens through technological progress. There are inventions that create huge shifts in productivity — wheel, steam power, electricity, radio, petroleum refinement, fission, transistors, internet, personal computing, and now, LLMs.

Humans have always automated their tasks. We have automated every boring and repetitive task that doesn't require unique human judgment. Technology enables that automation. The Luddites resisted. They lost.

White-collar jobs aren't immune from task automation. In the past 50 years, a lot of tasks done by knowledge workers have been automated. Think of all the typing pools, switchboard operators, filing clerks, human-calculators, bookkeepers, travel agents, floor traders, email-writing secretaries, code-writing programmers, and whatever you're doing right now.

A white-collar worker clocking 60-plus hours a week isn't doing 60-plus hours of high cognition tasks. No human can sustainably do so. A lot of the tasks require low intelligence — pulling data, formatting slides, cleaning spreadsheets, searching documents, researching information. Sitting behind a desk and working for 60-plus hours a week is the 1870s equivalent of Industrial Revolution workers toiling away for tasks that eventually got replaced by machines. Sitting behind a desk happens to require a college degree.

Knowledge work is a collection of tasks that relate to handling of information. Numerical data, code, written ideas, images, videos, etc. are all different forms of information. The tasks for working with information range from gathering (acquiring, researching), processing (transforming and creating), studying (understanding), committing (deciding, acting and communicating), and accountability (owning). The tasks fall on a spectrum. Within this spectrum, tasks that require gathering, processing and studying of information are more susceptible for automation than tasks that require committing and accountability.

AI can automate a lot of the tasks done by knowledge workers that involve information. AI can search through hundreds of documents to find the ones that matter, summarize them, extract data, make charts, write drafts, and understand the context around that information. Google democratized access to information. AI democratizes access to handling information. Gathering and processing of data, the grunt work, can now be automated.

No automation technology, including AI, has accountability in itself. All technology exists to help humans become more accountable. A human doing a job cannot hand off accountability of the tasks to technology. Even after inventing the loom, a human was responsible for not only operating it, but also crafting tasteful clothing from it. The market wasn't kind if the clothes came damaged or out of fashion because of the loom.

Accountability is always human. You can't sue, fire or shame an AI. It has no reputation to protect, no career to lose, no skin in the game. When AI becomes vastly more capable, the accountability function requires an entity that can be trusted, blamed, rewarded, or punished. That's a human. The core irreducible human core is the willingness to say "I stake my name on this" and mean it.

Where does human creativity, taste and judgement fit in? In committing and accountability. Acting on information requires agency, which AI lacks. A good act requires creativity and taste, otherwise the act is reduced to the average. Judgement is the precursor to being held accountable.

## AI and consulting

**Consulting**

Consulting is a mysterious profession where the core task is creating slides. Consultants do more than create pretty slides. Presentations are the standard delivery mechanisms for corporate America and consulting.

Consultants tell stories to convert organizational ambiguity into decisions. Everything else — the slides, frameworks, interviews, models — serves story telling.

**What do consultants really do?**

1. **Study & Recommend (Strategy)** This is the classic McKinsey/BCG/Bain engagement. Short, intense, senior-heavy. The consultant diagnoses a problem, builds an evidence-backed point of view, and delivers a recommendation.

2. **Teach & Facilitate (Capability Building)** The consultant embeds with the client to transfer skills, run workshops, coach leaders, and build internal muscle so the client doesn't need the consultant again.

3. **Build & Deliver (Implementation)** This is where consultants actually construct something — a new operating model, a technology platform, a restructured org, a migrated system. Strategy projects end when leadership aligns on a decision. Implementation projects end when outcomes are achieved.

4. **Staff & Augment (Body Shop)** This is the mode consultants don't love talking about. The client doesn't want advice, frameworks, or even a deliverable — they want warmies with specific skills to fill seats.

**Why are consultants hired?**

Consultants are hired when clients actively choose not to do stuff themselves, either because they can't or won't. There are five major reasons why consultants are hired:

- Capacity: Clients don't have time or bandwidth
- Expertise: Clients don't know specifics
- Political Cover: Clients need someone to say the hard truth internal politics won't let them
- Legitimacy: Clients need a brand name to justify their actions.
- Accountability: Clients need someone to share the risk

**Consultants sell via expertise and relationships**

Consulting is sold through two interlocking mechanisms: demonstrated expertise and compounding relationships. Consultants make themselves findable by showcasing deep expertise via thought leadership, speaking and convening, IP, and alumni networks. They never "sell consulting," rather, they aim to position themselves as the go-to person when an executive faces a problem they've chosen to not solve themselves. Consulting relationships compound over decades. The firm's alumni networks are very strong. That's why a consulting partner is mostly spending time on business development rather than actual client delivery.

**The Consulting model**

The traditional consulting staffing model is a pyramid. Partners at the top. Managers in the middle. Analysts at the bottom. This isn't an accident. It's economics.

The pyramid exists because grunt work is labor-intensive. Gathering data, processing spreadsheets, formatting slides, searching through documents, pulling numbers, making charts. All of this takes hours. Many hours. You need bodies to do it. So you hire many juniors to feed the machine. One partner bills out ten analysts. That's the leverage. That's how consulting prints money.

A consultant working 60-80 hours a week isn't doing 60-80 hours of high-cognition work. No human can. Much of those hours are spent on tasks that fall on the left side of the spectrum — gathering, processing, studying. The dull stuff. The stuff that AI can now do.

So what happens when AI eats the grunt work?

**The pyramid becomes a diamond.**

Fewer juniors at the bottom. Why hire ten analysts to pull data when AI can do it in minutes? The base of the pyramid shrinks. But the work doesn't disappear—it transforms. Someone still needs to judge whether the AI output makes sense. Someone needs to decide what to do with it. Someone needs to stand in front of the client and say, "I stake my reputation on this recommendation."

That's the middle of the diamond. More mid-level humans. But not the same humans. Not MBAs fresh out of school grinding 60 hours on data pulls. Industry veterans. Domain experts. People with 10-20 years of actual experience who can look at AI-generated analysis and know, immediately, whether it's right or wrong. People who have the judgment that only comes from doing the work for decades.

At the top? Seniors who own relationships and bear accountability. This doesn't change much. Partners will still be partners. They sell, they own clients, they take the blame when things go wrong.

<PyramidToDiamond />

The AI agents don't replace the human structure. They run parallel to it. Think of it as two pyramids of AI sitting on either side of the human diamond, augmenting every level. Juniors supervise AI that gathers. Mid-levels supervise AI that processes and synthesizes. Seniors use AI to stress-test recommendations before they stake their name on them.

The type of consultant changes. The shape of the firm changes. The tasks change. The job remains.

If you want a preview of this future, look at software engineering. Claude Code didn't eliminate engineers. It eliminated the dull parts of engineering—the boilerplate, the syntax errors, the repetitive patterns. What's left is the pure essence of the job: pragmatically solving problems. The engineers who remain are more leveraged, not less. They do more with less time. They focus on what humans do best.

Consulting is next.
